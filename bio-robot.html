Bio-Inspired Subterranean Robot

Designed and developed a worm-inspired subterranean robot to measure underground CO₂ concentration in agricultural pipe networks.

Mechanical Design Sensors & Locomotion CPG-Based Control 3D Reconstruction

1. Overview

Designed and developed a worm-inspired subterranean robot to measure underground CO₂ concentration in agricultural pipe networks, enabling environmental analysis to study its potential effect on crop yield.

The system integrates articulated locomotion, live environmental sensing, and vision-based 3D reconstruction for subsurface mapping.

2. Problem Statement

Underground agricultural pipelines are narrow, grooved, and include sharp bends. Traditional inspection systems cannot navigate these constrained geometries while collecting environmental data.

The objective was to:

  • Traverse narrow underground pipes
  • Handle bends up to 90°
  • Collect real-time CO₂ data
  • Reconstruct internal pipe geometry

3. Mechanical Design

The robot was designed as a dual-module worm-inspired system with an articulated universal joint at the center.

I engineered:

  • A custom geared articulated joint
  • A bending mechanism capable of 90° turns
  • Wheg-based traction extrusions to grip grooved pipe interiors

The geared joint allowed adaptive bending while maintaining structural stability inside curved pipe sections.

4. Locomotion Strategy – CPG-Based Synchronization

To achieve coordinated motion inside confined geometry, I implemented a Central Pattern Generator (CPG)-inspired synchronization between the front and rear modules.

This enabled:

  • Phase-shifted rhythmic motion
  • Stable propulsion in constrained environments
  • Continuous traction during bending

5. Sensing & Mapping

The system integrates:

  • SCD30 CO₂ sensor for environmental monitoring
  • Raspberry Pi (compact form factor) for onboard processing
  • Live data transmission
  • Camera-based 3D reconstruction for internal pipe mapping

The 3D reconstruction pipeline estimated pipe curvature and bend angle to assist navigation decisions.

6. Experimental Validation

The robot was physically tested in grooved underground pipe structures.

Results:

  • Validated mechanical adaptability in curved sections
